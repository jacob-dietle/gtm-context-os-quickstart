# Voice Calibration Reference

---

## The Voice Floor Principle

This is NOT a style guide to imitate. It is a set of constraints to prevent the output from reading like AI wrote it.

**Goal:** Clean prose the author can work with. Not mimicry.

**The test:** If you read the draft and can't tell whether a human or AI wrote a given paragraph, it passes. If any paragraph screams "AI generated this," it fails.

---

## Calibrating to YOUR Voice

To make this skill produce content that sounds like you, add observations about your own writing below. Read 5-10 of your published pieces and note patterns in these categories:

### Register
- How formal/casual is your default tone?
- Do you use contractions? Fragments? Slang?
- What does your writing sound like — explaining to a friend? Teaching a class? Presenting to a board?

### Paragraph Shape
- How long are your typical paragraphs? (Count sentences in 5 random paragraphs from your work.)
- Do you mix lengths or stay consistent?

### How Ideas Connect
- Do you use formal transitions ("Furthermore", "Moreover") or just start the next thought?
- How do you bridge between sections?

### Claims and Opinions
- Do you state positions directly or hedge?
- How do you handle uncertainty — vague disclaimers or specific acknowledgment of what you don't know?

### Examples and Evidence
- Do you use personal examples or hypothetical scenarios?
- How technical do you get?

### Humor (if applicable)
- What kind? Dry? Self-deprecating? None?

**Fill these sections out by reading your own published work, not by describing how you'd like to sound.** What you actually write is different from what you think you write.

---

## Voice Floor Rules (Enforce These)

These are universal — they prevent AI-sounding output regardless of the author's personal style.

1. **No formal transitions.** Ever. Just start the next thought.
2. **No hedge words.** State it or don't. "Might potentially" is never the answer.
3. **No passive voice.** Active voice, direct sentences.
4. **No sycophantic framing.** Nothing is "groundbreaking" or "revolutionary."
5. **No abstract framing.** No "paradox", "tension", "dichotomy."
6. **Short paragraphs.** Max 4 sentences. Break it up.
7. **Contractions always.** "Do not" -> "don't". "It is" -> "it's".
8. **"You" not "organizations."** Direct address, not corporate distance.
9. **No signposted structure.** Don't say "In this section we will discuss..." — just discuss it.
10. **No wrapping-up language.** Don't say "In conclusion" or "To sum up." End with the strongest claim or the most interesting open question.

---

## What to Leave for the Author

Some things this skill should NOT attempt:

- **Personal anecdotes.** Leave `[AUTHOR: your example of X here]` placeholders.
- **Hot takes.** The skill can frame the setup, but the opinion itself should come from the author.
- **Humor.** Don't try to be funny. Clean neutral prose is better than failed jokes.
- **Casual asides.** If the author has a distinctive style of aside, don't fake it.

The skill's job is to produce a clean, well-structured, evidence-backed draft that the author can inject their personality into. Not to perform their personality for them.
